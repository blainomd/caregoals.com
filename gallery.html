Dorothy's wish

Dorothy is 82. Sharp, independent, and she'd been living alone in her house on Mapleton for twelve years after her husband died. Her daughter Jess had been gently trying to talk about what would happen "when things changed" for two years — and Dorothy would change the subject.

When Jess sat down with Dorothy and opened CareGoals, she framed it as "a thing I need to do for myself too." Sage asked Dorothy what a good day looked like. She talked for fifteen minutes — her garden, her book club, the way the light comes through the kitchen window in the morning. Nobody had asked her that question in years.

"I don't want any heroics. I've had a beautiful life. What I want is to die in my garden if I can manage it, with my family nearby. I just needed someone to ask."

— Dorothy, 82

Three brothers

When their father had a stroke at 77, Michael, David, and Tom had to make decisions for him — and they disagreed about everything. Michael wanted aggressive treatment. David wanted comfort care. Tom wasn't sure. There was no document, no prior conversation, and it nearly broke the family.

Their father recovered enough to do a CareGoals session himself six months later. The three brothers sat with him while Sage asked questions. By the time they finished, all three were in tears — not because of the hard topics, but because they'd never heard their father talk about his life like that before.

"I just want to be comfortable. I don't want to be a burden. I want my boys to be okay. That's it."

— Their father, 78
What changed
  • check_circleAll three brothers aligned on care preferences
  • check_circleComfort care preference documented
  • check_circleSummary shared with their father's neurologist

Before the diagnosis

Karen and her husband Robert did their CareGoals sessions the summer he turned 68 — "just to have it done," as Karen put it. No health crisis. No urgency. Just a Tuesday evening and a bottle of wine.

When Robert was diagnosed with early Parkinson's two years later, they weren't starting from scratch. The plan was already there. The conversation had already happened. What might have been a devastating moment of renegotiating everything was instead a calm update to something they'd already talked through together.

"We did it before we needed to. That felt strange at the time. Now it feels like the smartest thing we ever did."

— Karen, 67

A caregiver's perspective

Maria has been a home caregiver for eleven years. She's been in the room when family members have had to make decisions for someone who couldn't speak for themselves — and she's seen what happens when there's no plan.

"The families who've done this are different," she says. "They're still sad. They still grieve. But they're not fighting each other. They know what their person wanted. That changes everything."

"I recommend CareGoals to every family I work with. I tell them: don't wait for a reason. The reason is that you love them."

— Maria, co-op.care caregiver
