What matters most

Sage starts here — before any medical questions. Understanding what makes life meaningful shapes every answer that follows. These aren't hypotheticals. They're about right now.

"Describe what a really good day feels like for you right now."

"A slow morning — coffee before anyone needs anything from me. A walk. Reading. Something creative in the afternoon. Ending the day having talked to someone I love."

"What activities or experiences are so important to you that life wouldn't feel like yours without them?"

"Gardening. Cooking Sunday dinners for my family. Following my grandchildren's lives closely."

"What does independence mean to you, specifically?"

"Being able to decide my own schedule. Not needing to ask permission for things I've always managed myself. Staying in my own home."

Medical decisions

These are the questions families face at 2am in the ICU. Sage asks them in plain language, grounded in what the person already told Sage they value. The reasoning behind the answer matters as much as the answer itself.

"If you were seriously ill and the medical team offered a treatment that might help but would mean weeks in the hospital — how would you weigh that decision?"

"It would depend on what 'might help' actually means. I'd want to know the realistic odds. I'm not afraid of discomfort if there's real hope. But I don't want to spend my last weeks in a hospital bed for a 5% chance."

"What does 'quality of life' mean to you, concretely?"

"Being able to recognize my family. Having some control over my day. Not being in constant pain. Those are the things that make life feel like mine."

"If you could no longer communicate, what would you most want your care team to know about how to treat you?"

"That I value being talked to, not talked about. That even if I can't respond, I may still be aware. That comfort and dignity matter more to me than extending time."

How you want to live

Where you are, what surrounds you, and how a caregiver should treat you — these aren't luxuries. When someone can no longer advocate for themselves, these details prevent months of family debate and let caregivers provide genuinely personal care.

"If you needed ongoing care, where would you most want to receive it?"

"Home, unquestionably. My own bedroom. My own kitchen. Even if I needed someone there all day — I'd want it to be in my home."

"What would your ideal care environment feel like on a typical day?"

"Quiet in the morning. Visitors I actually want to see in the afternoon. A caregiver who feels more like a neighbor than an employee. Music, natural light, my own things around me."

"Are there things about your daily routine that are non-negotiable to you?"

"Morning coffee before anyone talks to me. A window I can look out of. My garden nearby if possible. These aren't luxuries — they're what makes life feel continuous."

Who speaks for you

A healthcare proxy is the person who speaks for you when you can't. This is among the most consequential designations in advance care planning — and it's often undermined by family conflict when it hasn't been explicitly named and explained.

"Who in your life knows you well enough to make hard decisions on your behalf — and would actually make the choice you'd make?"

"My son David. We've talked about this. He doesn't flinch from hard things. He knows I wouldn't want to be kept alive if there was no meaningful chance of recovery. He'd honor that, even if it was painful for him."

"What do you want your healthcare proxy to know that they might not already know?"

"That I've thought about this. That my preferences aren't a crisis response — I've been at peace with this for years. I want them to act without guilt."

"Are there family members who might challenge decisions your proxy makes — and have you prepared for that?"

"My daughter and I don't always see eye to eye on this. She's more 'do everything possible.' I need to have a real conversation with her before anything happens. This process is helping me realize I've been putting that off."

Your legacy

This section is different from the others. It's not about decisions — it's about meaning. Families who complete this section often describe it as the most emotionally significant part. It turns a care planning document into something that endures.

"What do you want people to say about you at your memorial — not the achievements, but the way you made them feel?"

"That I showed up. That I was the person people called when something hard happened. That I made things a little less scary."

"Is there something important about your life that most people don't know?"

"That I almost became a musician. I spent two years playing in a jazz band in my twenties. I gave it up for practical reasons. Music has been the private language of my inner life ever since."

"What values do you most want to pass on — to your children, grandchildren, the people you've loved?"

"Curiosity. The belief that learning doesn't stop. And a kind of stubborn faith that things can get better — that the future is worth showing up for."
